# Business & Admin Layer

*The infrastructure the rest of the plan quietly assumes. Not glamorous, but load-bearing: the bankroll rules can't run without clean records, and a self-employed 45-year-old getting surgery needs coverage handled before the OR. A note up front — I'm not a tax, legal, or financial advisor. This is the landscape and the right specialists to hire, not advice. Where it says "ask the CPA," that's the real instruction.*

---

## Why this is load-bearing, not optional

Three things make this track non-skippable this year:

1. **The bankroll engine depends on it.** Every move-up / move-down / shot-take rule is gated on knowing your *real* roll and your *real* win rate by format and stake. No tracking → the whole framework is guesswork, and game selection (where your edge actually pays) is blind.
2. **The tax law changed against you in 2026** (details below). Clean records went from housekeeping to the thing that protects you from being taxed on money you never made.
3. **You've already got the hard pieces.** The specialist CPA and the insurance are handled — the two things hardest to acquire. So what's left here isn't a build from scratch; it's execution: a tracking system you actually keep, and a CPA you use proactively instead of once a year.

---

## 1. Results tracking — the spine

The single most important admin habit. Log **every** session: date, venue/site, game, stake, buy-in, cash-out, hours.

- **Live:** manual discipline — log it the same night, before you've rounded it off in your memory. An app or a clean spreadsheet, whichever you'll actually keep up.
- **Online:** database tracking where the site/format supports it (spotty for PLO and mixed games), manual everywhere else.
- **The payoff stacks:** real win rates by format feed the bankroll rules *and* the skills track (which edges to lean into), and clean tax records fall out for free. This is also exactly what the app's tracker view becomes, and what the Sunday review reads from.

This is the keystone of the whole track. Build it in Phase 1 and never let it lapse.

## 2. Taxes — and the 2026 change that hits you specifically

*You already have a gambling-expert CPA — so treat this section as the agenda to put in front of them, not homework to do alone.*

**The headline:** under the OBBBA, starting tax year 2026, gambling losses are only **90% deductible** against winnings (down from 100%). The "losses can't exceed winnings, and can't carry forward" rules stay.

**Why it bites a tournament pro hard — "phantom income":** if you win $250k and lose $250k across the year — dead break-even — you can only deduct $225k, so you're taxed on $25k you never actually pocketed. A losing or break-even year can now generate a tax bill. High-variance, high-volume tournament play is the worst-case profile for this rule.

**What it means for you:**
- You almost certainly file as a **professional** — gambling as a business on **Schedule C**, which lets you deduct expenses (travel, training, a share of buy-ins, etc.) but owes self-employment tax. Exactly how the 90% limit nets out for a Schedule C pro is genuinely unsettled and complex — which is the whole reason the next point matters.
- **You have the specialist — now use them proactively.** The leverage isn't *having* the CPA, it's bringing them the hard questions *before* WSOP rather than at filing time: how the 90% rule nets against your specific volume and action-selling, how large your quarterly estimates need to be, and the multi-state picture. Book the working session in Phase 1.
- **Quarterly estimated taxes** — set the money aside as you go; don't get caught in April.
- **Reporting thresholds rose:** the W-2G threshold went to $2,000 for 2026 (inflation-indexed going forward). Doesn't change what you owe, just when forms get issued.
- **Multi-state:** live play in DE and MD plus WSOP in NV means out-of-state winnings and multi-state filing. Some states don't allow loss deductions at all. The CPA handles this.
- **Watch the politics, don't plan around them:** there's an active push (led partly by Nevada lawmakers) to reverse the 90% rule, possibly retroactive to Jan 1 2026. Plan under current law, keep great records, and let the upside be a bonus if it lands.

## 3. Staking / action-sale accounting — the real trap

Selling ~$130k of action across the WSOP slate isn't a handshake — it's accounting, and it interacts badly with the new tax rule.

- **The trap:** when you cash, the casino reports the **full** prize under your name and SSN. You then distribute backers' shares. Their money is **not** your income — but you only get to treat it that way if you've documented the agreements cleanly. Sloppy records and you risk being taxed on the whole cash, including money you handed straight to backers. Layer the 90% loss cap on top and the math gets genuinely treacherous.
- **What protects you:** written staking agreements (markup, % sold, makeup terms), a clean per-entry ledger of who owns what, and a CPA who structures the reporting correctly. A reputable staking platform can help with the paper trail.
- **Timing:** the "secure backing" task in Phase 3–4 includes the *paperwork*, not just the commitments. Don't reconstruct it in July.

## 4. Health insurance — covered, one check left

You're already insured — which removes the scariest time-sensitive item on this list. What remains is a phone call, not a project:

- **Confirm the surgery is covered before you book it.** Check that the hernia repair and the colonoscopy are in-network and covered, and know your deductible and out-of-pocket max for the year, so the Phase 1 medical block has no billing surprises.
- That's it. The hard part — being a self-employed 45-year-old *with* real coverage heading into an operation — is already done.

## 5. Banking & fund segregation

Mirror the bankroll buckets with **real, separate accounts**, so discipline is structural rather than willpower:

- **Playing roll** · **WSOP fund** · **Taxes** (park your tax % out of every win — it was never yours) · **Life** (the separate income that covers expenses).

Segregation is what stops you from accidentally spending the tax money or dipping the WSOP fund on a Tuesday. It also makes the bookkeeping and the CPA's job dramatically cleaner.

---

## 6. Retirement & wealth outside poker — the 45 lens

The roll is working capital. It is *not* a retirement plan, and at 45 with a volatile income, a base **outside** poker is part of "a sustainable life that holds."

- **Self-employed retirement vehicles** — SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) — are tax-advantaged and built for exactly your situation. Setting one up also reduces taxable income.
- A **fee-only** financial advisor (flat fee, not commission) can help build the non-poker base. (Category, not a recommendation — I'm not a financial advisor.)
- This is the quiet long-game most grinders skip and regret. You're thinking in years now anyway — extend it past the felt.

---

## 7. Entity / structure — settled

You file as a professional on **Schedule C** with **no LLC** — the standard and usually-correct setup for pure poker income; entities tend to complicate it rather than help. Leave it as is. The only reason to revisit: if the **solver project becomes a product** or the **staking side grows into its own business**, an LLC to house *that* (not the poker) becomes a one-line question for your CPA. Not now.

- **Phase 6 (Landing):** reconcile the year, settle backers, hand the CPA clean books.
